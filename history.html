The Line

The City That Kept Going

The crisis isn't something anyone aboard discusses much. It happened, the ship left, and life had to continue anyway, inside a hull, on a ten-year clock, with the technology it departed with and nothing more. What holds that life together is MetroTech: not the band first, but the civic transit and broadcast network the band took its name from and now broadcasts across. Relay operators, reactor techs, teachers, dockhands, kids cutting class, everyone rides the same line, under the same fluorescent hum, whether they're headed to a shift or nowhere in particular. It's the ship's lifeblood, the thing that makes a fortress-city function like a city instead of a machine.

Ride it station to station and you don't just cross the ship, you cross the way people have learned to live on it. Ten districts, ten different tempos, all sharing one spine.

The Stops

Ten Districts, One Hull

Every stop on the line is its own small civilization. Drop an image in for each once you've got one, the frame is already built.

Stop 01
Promenade Deck

The ship's main commercial walkway, always a little too bright. Off-shift crowds drift past storefronts selling the same dozen things in new colors, comfort, mostly, dressed up as commerce.

Stop 02
Signal Lounge Quarter

Performance spaces and late bars stacked three decks deep. Musicians, dreamers, and the regulars who never quite leave, this is where MetroTech was born, long before it had a name.

Stop 03
Transit Glass Sector

Long reflective corridors and commuter flow that never fully stops. Nobody's culture, exactly, everyone's, for the length of a ride.

Stop 04
Mall Sector

Arcades and neon storefronts running promotions for products that stopped shipping years before departure. Teenagers still treat it like the center of the universe. Some things don't change.

Stop 05
Rain Grid

Indoor rain gardens and a sky that's programmed, not real. Where Dani runs the dawn cycle, and where the ship goes to remember what weather used to feel like.

Stop 06
Panoramic Deck

Massive viewing glass and a silence nobody enforces. People come here to look at stars that don't care what time the ship has decided it is, and to feel small in the good way for a while.

Stop 07
Reactor Level

Immense humming machinery, treated by the people who work it like something closer to a temple than a power plant. Sable's territory, the ship as an instrument, if you know how to listen.

Stop 08
Blue CRT Block

Decommissioned decks nobody officially uses anymore. The lights still flicker on schedule, out of habit. Explorers, scavengers, and teenagers looking for somewhere the administration forgot about.

Stop 09
Broadcast Sector

Antenna farms and civic transmission infrastructure, humming overhead like something breathing. The dead channel MetroTech broadcasts on lives somewhere in here.

Stop 10
Sector Lines

Where people actually live. Quiet, ordinary, and the only district that was never meant to be seen by anyone just passing through, just home, however far from home it actually is.

Origin

Where They Met

Before MetroTech existed, there were just three separate paths through Signal Lounge Quarter. Serin came because her bunk was too quiet and the lounge wasn't. Dani sat alone near the speaker stack most nights, humming along to a house system nobody was collecting royalties on. Sable ran sound for a duo that broke up before most regulars ever caught their name.

None of them planned to meet. They just kept showing up, night after night, until they were fixtures before they were friends, and friends before they were a band. Somewhere in those overlapping nights at Signal Lounge, three people who happened to like the same room became the thing this city didn't know it was missing.

"We became a fixture before we became a band." From the ship-log entries, Signal Lounge Quarter

That's why the debut record carries the name Signal Lounge Ensemble, not just where they met, but the place that turned three separate signals into one.
